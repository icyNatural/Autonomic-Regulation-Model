# Autonomic Regulation Model

## Overview
The body continuously regulates itself through interacting processes that support action and recovery. These processes are not mutually exclusive; both are active at all times, with varying levels of influence depending on context.

## Regulation Processes

### Sympathetic Influence (Mobilization)
- Associated with action, response, and energy expenditure
- Breathing tends to become faster and more shallow
- Attention narrows toward relevant stimuli
- Supports movement, decision-making, and rapid adjustment

### Parasympathetic Influence (Restoration)
- Associated with maintenance, conservation, and internal regulation
- Breathing tends to become slower and more rhythmic
- Heart rate variability often increases
- Supports digestion, repair, and systemic balance

## Balance
These influences operate together rather than as a strict switch. The body adjusts their relative contribution based on environmental demands, internal state, and ongoing activity.

## Modulating Inputs

### Breathing
Changes in breathing pattern can influence autonomic balance. Slower, more regular breathing is commonly associated with increased parasympathetic influence, while faster breathing patterns are often associated with increased sympathetic influence.

### Rhythm (e.g., music)
Consistent external rhythms can act as a reference signal for internal timing processes, which may influence breathing patterns and overall regulation.

### Cognitive Load (e.g., language, interpretation)
Engagement in interpretation or complex thought can shift attention toward cognitive processing. Depending on intensity and context, this may alter the balance between mobilization and restoration processes.

## Core Observation
Autonomic regulation is a continuous balancing process influenced by internal rhythms, external inputs, and cognitive activity.
