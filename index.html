Autonomic Regulation Model

A neutral systems view describing how the body balances mobilization and restoration through continuous autonomic regulation, shaped by rhythm, breathing, and cognitive load.

Continuous Regulation

Core System

These processes are not strict opposites that switch on and off. Both remain active, while their relative influence shifts with context, demand, and internal state.

Relative Influence Dynamic, not binary
Sympathetic Mobilization
Parasympathetic Restoration
Mobilization supports action, response, and energy expenditure
Restoration supports maintenance, repair, and internal balance

Sympathetic Influence

Mobilization

  • Associated with action, response, and energy expenditure
  • Breathing tends to become faster and more shallow
  • Attention narrows toward relevant stimuli
  • Supports movement, decision-making, and rapid adjustment

Parasympathetic Influence

Restoration

  • Associated with maintenance, conservation, and internal regulation
  • Breathing tends to become slower and more rhythmic
  • Heart rate variability often increases
  • Supports digestion, repair, and systemic balance

Modulating Inputs

Breathing
Changes in breathing pattern can influence autonomic balance. Slower, more regular breathing is commonly associated with increased parasympathetic influence.
→
Rhythm
Consistent external rhythm can act as a reference signal for internal timing processes and may influence overall regulation.
→
Cognitive Load
Engagement in interpretation or complex thought can shift the balance depending on intensity, context, and sustained demand.
